Why Power Delivery Matters for ML SoCs

the hardest problem nobody outside silicon talks about

700–1200 WTDP of modern AI accelerators (B200, MI300X)
600+ APeak current at 0.75 V core supply
<3%Voltage noise budget (±22 mV at 0.75 V)
100+ A/nsdi/dt transient during workload switching

Why It Is Getting Harder

  • Lower Vdd — sub-0.8 V rails shrink absolute noise margin
  • Higher current density — >2 A/mm² in modern logic tiles
  • Bursty workloads — GEMM→all-reduce transitions create massive di/dt
  • Larger die / multi-die — reticle-limit dies and chiplet assemblies lengthen PDN
  • Thermal co-stress — hot spots raise local resistance, worsening IR drop

Consequences of Poor Power Delivery

  • Timing failures — voltage droop slows gates, violates setup constraints
  • Frequency guardband — 10–15% clock reduction to tolerate worst-case droop
  • Electromigration — J > J_crit accelerates void formation (Black’s law)
  • Reliability — repeated thermal cycling fatigues solder bumps/pillars
  • Power waste — voltage guardbands × current = wasted Watts
Every 1% reduction in voltage guardband for a 1000 W accelerator recovers ~10 W — equivalent to a meaningful gain in TOPS/W for inference workloads.

PDN Impedance & Target Impedance

frequency-domain view of power delivery quality

Z_target = V_ripple / I_transient   —   e.g. Z_target = 22.5 mV / 600 A = 37.5 µΩ

Target Impedance Method

  • Flat impedance — maintain Z < Z_target from DC to ~1 GHz
  • Anti-resonance peaks — occur at transitions between cap stages; must be damped below target
  • Series resonance of each cap stage creates a “valley” in Z(f) where that stage dominates
  • Parallel resonance between adjacent stages creates dangerous peaks
  • Analysis via S-parameter measurements or 3D EM extraction tools (Ansys SIwave, Cadence Sigrity)
For a 1000 W SoC at 0.75 V with 3% noise margin: Z_target = 0.0225 / 1333 = 16.9 µΩ — an extraordinarily low impedance to maintain across decades of frequency.

Anti-Resonance Mitigation

  • Overlap ESR-damped capacitor values to smooth transitions
  • Use controlled-ESR capacitors to flatten peaks
  • Distribute package caps spatially to reduce mutual inductance
  • eDTC on-die capacitance pushes mid-frequency Z below target

IR Drop Analysis

static vs dynamic drop, current density, and electromigration

Static IR Drop

  • V_drop = I × R_path from pad to transistor
  • Dominated by metal stack sheet resistance (Cu at 5nm: R_sheet ~50–100 mΩ/sq for M1)
  • Worst at die center — longest path from peripheral power bumps
  • Typical budget: 20–40 mV static drop (3–5% of Vdd)
  • Mitigation: thicker upper metals (M12–M15 at 3–5 µm), denser bump grid, BSPDN

Dynamic IR Drop

  • V_drop = L × di/dt + I × R — inductive component dominates during transients
  • Triggered by simultaneous switching of large logic blocks
  • Can exceed static drop by 2–5× during worst-case events
  • Duration: typically 1–50 ns droop events
  • Mitigation: decoupling, staggered clock enables, power-aware placement

Electromigration & Black's Equation

MTTF = A × J−n × exp(E_a / k_B T)
  • J — current density (A/cm²); n ≈ 1–2 for Cu
  • E_a — activation energy (~0.7–0.9 eV for Cu dual-damascene)
  • T — temperature; 10°C rise → ~2× EM rate acceleration
  • J_max for Cu at 105°C: ~1–2 MA/cm² (varies by process / metal level)
  • Power grid wires must be sized for EM limits, not just IR drop

Current Density Maps

  • Generated via vectorless or VCD-based dynamic analysis
  • Hot spots correlate with high-activity macro blocks (tensor cores, SRAM banks)
  • Tools: Cadence Voltus, Synopsys RedHawk, Ansys Totem
  • Signoff criteria: max dynamic droop < 8–10% Vdd, EM margin >10 years at 105°C

VRM Topologies for AI Accelerators

multi-phase buck converters, DrMOS, and transient response

Multi-Phase Buck Converter

  • Topology — N interleaved buck phases (typically 12–20 phases for >600A)
  • Switching freq — 400–800 kHz per phase; effective ripple at N×f_sw
  • Current sharing — DCR sensing or hall-effect; <5% imbalance target
  • Efficiency — 90–93% at full load for 12V→0.75V conversion
  • Transient response — droop during 0→full-load step: V_droop = L_out × di/dt
  • Output filter — L: 50–150 nH/phase, C_out: 10–30 mF total

48V Direct Conversion

  • OCP Open Rack V3: 48V bus eliminates 12V intermediate stage
  • 4:1 ratio — 48V → ~12V via switched-cap, then multi-phase buck to Vcore
  • Or direct 48V→0.75V using high-ratio topologies (Vicor, Infineon)
  • Reduces I²R losses in PCB (4× lower current at 48V vs 12V)
  • NVIDIA HGX B200: 48V input, ~1200W per GPU module

DrMOS & Smart Power Stages

  • DrMOS: Driver + high-side + low-side FETs in single package (8×8 mm)
  • Infineon TDA21490: 90A continuous, R_DS(on) < 0.5 mΩ
  • Renesas ISL99390: 90A, integrated temperature sensing
  • GaN power stages — higher f_sw (2–4 MHz), smaller inductors, faster transient
  • EPC2302: GaN FET, 1.3 mΩ, enables >2 MHz switching

On-Package Voltage Regulation (PIVR)

Intel FIVR and the push to integrate regulation into the package

Intel Fully Integrated Voltage Regulator (FIVR)

  • First deployed in Haswell (2013); refined through Alder Lake, Meteor Lake
  • Topology — on-die multi-phase buck with thin-film inductors on package
  • Switching frequency: 60–140 MHz (100× higher than board VRM)
  • Enables per-core DVFS with <100 ns voltage transition
  • Inductor: air-core thin-film, ~1–2 nH, embedded in package substrate
  • Efficiency: 85–90% (trades ~3% vs board VRM for faster response)
FIVR eliminates the PCB-to-die impedance for the regulated domain. The VRM output is inside the package — reducing PDN inductance by 10–50× versus board regulation. This translates directly to reduced voltage guardband.

Advantages Over Board VRM

  • Faster transient response — 100 ns vs 10–50 µs for board VRM
  • Per-domain regulation — separate rails for each core, GPU, IO, etc.
  • Reduced guardband — 30–50 mV savings enables higher frequency or lower power
  • Fewer board components — simpler motherboard, smaller form factor
  • Dynamic power savings — rapid DVFS during idle/burst cycles

PIVR in AI Accelerators

  • Intel Ponte Vecchio — FIVR with per-tile regulation across 47 chiplets
  • Intel Gaudi 3 — FIVR for independent HBM and compute voltage domains
  • Package inductors: 2.5D embedded thin-film on EMIB interposer
  • Trade-off: FIVR consumes ~2–4% die area for power FETs + control

On-Die LDO & Integrated Voltage Regulators

granular per-block DVFS with fully on-die regulation

On-Die LDO (Low Dropout Regulator)

  • Topology — PMOS pass transistor + error amplifier, fully in standard CMOS
  • Dropout — 50–100 mV minimum (V_in − V_out)
  • Efficiency — η = V_out / V_in ≈ 85–93% (limited by dropout)
  • Bandwidth — loop BW up to 100–500 MHz — excellent transient response
  • Area — ~0.01–0.05 mm² per LDO instance, distributed across die
  • Use case — per-core, per-SRAM-bank, or per-tensor-unit voltage islands

Digital LDO

  • Array of parallel PMOS switches with binary-weighted widths
  • Digital comparator + shift register replaces analog error amp
  • Advantage — fully synthesizable, scales with process node
  • Disadvantage — limit-cycle oscillation, slower response than analog LDO
  • Hybrid designs combine coarse digital + fine analog paths

On-Die Switched-Cap IVR

  • Charge-pump topology: 2:1 or 3:2 ratio conversion using on-die MOS caps
  • Efficiency — >90% at fixed rational conversion ratios
  • No inductors required — fully CMOS compatible
  • Flying capacitors: MOS-cap or MIM-cap in BEOL stack
  • Limitation — fixed ratio; cascaded with LDO for fine regulation
  • Apple M-series: switched-cap + LDO for per-cluster voltage islands

BSPDN — Backside Power Delivery Network

Intel PowerVia, TSMC N2P — decoupling power from signal routing

How BSPDN Works

  • Conventional (frontside) — power & signal share the same BEOL metal stack
  • BSPDN — power rails delivered through the backside of the wafer via nano-TSVs
  • Wafer is thinned to ~1–3 µm, backside metallization added (2–4 metal layers)
  • Nano-TSVs (~50–200 nm diameter) connect backside power to frontside transistors
  • Signal routing uses full BEOL stack — no power rails consuming M1/M2 tracks

Intel PowerVia (Intel 20A / 18A)

  • First production BSPDN: Intel 20A (2024)
  • Backside power with RibbonFET (gate-all-around) transistors
  • 30–50% IR drop reduction vs frontside at iso-density
  • 6–10% frequency uplift from reduced voltage guardband
  • Cell height reduction: enables 6T SRAM at higher density

Benefits of BSPDN

  • IR drop — 30–50% reduction; shorter, wider power paths to transistors
  • Signal routing — 15–25% more tracks available (no VDD/VSS on M1/M2)
  • Cell density — tighter standard cell pitch; potential 10–20% area reduction
  • Decoupling — backside metal planes act as distributed capacitance
  • Thermal — direct backside metal contact to heat sink / cold plate

eDTC — Embedded Deep Trench Capacitors

TSMC's on-die capacitance revolution for PDN impedance

What is eDTC?

  • Deep trenches etched into the silicon substrate, lined with high-k dielectric and filled with conductor
  • Capacitance density: 250–400 nF/mm² (vs ~20 nF/mm² for MOS-cap filler)
  • 10–20× higher capacitance density than conventional on-die MOS decaps
  • Trenches are ~5–10 µm deep, ~100–200 nm wide, etched into Si substrate
  • Process: adapted from DRAM deep trench technology

Impact on PDN Impedance

  • 2–5× reduction in on-die PDN impedance at 200 MHz–2 GHz
  • Pushes anti-resonance peaks below target impedance
  • Enables flatter Z(f) profile across critical frequency band
  • Reduces need for package-level decoupling caps (cost saving)
  • Particularly impactful for large-die ML accelerators with high di/dt

Through-Silicon Via Power Delivery

TSV arrays for 2.5D/3D stacking — current capacity, thermal co-design

TSV Power Delivery Basics

  • TSV diameter — 5–10 µm (standard), 1–3 µm (fine-pitch for 3D)
  • Resistance per TSV — ~10–50 mΩ (depending on diameter/depth)
  • Current per TSV — 10–50 mA per via at EM limits
  • Pitch — 30–100 µm; dense arrays needed for high-current delivery
  • For 500A total: need ~10,000–50,000 power TSVs

2.5D Interposer PDN (CoWoS)

  • TSMC CoWoS-S: silicon interposer with TSVs connecting package to chiplets
  • Interposer TSVs: 10 µm diameter, 100 µm depth, ~25 mΩ each
  • Power planes in interposer RDL layers (3–4 metal layers)
  • Decoupling capacitors can be placed on interposer (MIM caps)
  • Total power TSV count for HBM+GPU assembly: 50,000+

3D Stacking Power Challenges

  • Thermal stacking — top die heated by bottom die; T_junction rises → higher R → worse IR drop
  • TSV area overhead — 5–15% of die area consumed by TSV keep-out zones
  • Reliability — Cu TSV pumping, thermo-mechanical stress, Cu/oxide delamination
  • IR drop compounds — power must traverse multiple die layers in series
  • AMD 3D V-Cache: ~100k TSVs for power + signal between CCD and cache die

di/dt Management in ML Training Workloads

GEMM→communication switching, droop events, firmware mitigation

The di/dt Problem in ML Training

  • GEMM phase — all tensor cores active, ~100% utilization, peak current draw
  • Communication phase — NVLink/ICI all-reduce; tensor cores idle, current drops 40–60%
  • Transition — 500–1000A swing in <1 µs → massive Ldi/dt voltage droop
  • Droop magnitude: 50–100 mV for worst-case transitions
  • Occurs predictably at layer boundaries in transformer training
  • Frequency: every 100–500 µs during typical LLM training iterations
A 1000A swing in 500 ns with 50 pH effective PDN inductance produces V_droop = L × di/dt = 50 pH × 2×10&sup9; A/s = 100 mV droop — exceeding the entire noise budget.

Hardware Mitigation

  • On-die decoupling — eDTC + MOS caps provide charge reservoir for <50 ns events
  • Staggered clock gating — spread wake-up across 10–50 ns window
  • Current-step limiters — on-die circuits that limit max di/dt by throttling clock enables
  • Adaptive clocking — drooping clock tracks supply voltage, maintaining timing margins
  • PIVR/FIVR — fast VRM response (<1 µs) catches medium-duration droops

Firmware & Software Mitigation

  • Workload scheduling — overlap compute and communication to smooth transitions
  • DVFS pre-positioning — lower voltage before known idle → reduce droop magnitude
  • Droop predictor — ML model predicting upcoming di/dt events, pre-activating mitigations
  • Power throttling — firmware caps max current ramp rate during phase transitions
  • NVIDIA GPU firmware: dynamic power balancing across SMs to limit aggregate di/dt

Analysis Flow

Power Delivery in Chiplet Architectures

per-chiplet regulation, interposer PDN, power gating coordination

Chiplet PDN Challenges

  • Multiple voltage domains — compute, IO, HBM PHY each need distinct Vdd
  • Cross-chiplet transients — one chiplet's di/dt couples through shared interposer PDN
  • Interposer IR drop — silicon interposer adds 5–20 mΩ series resistance per path
  • Power bump density — limited by µ-bump pitch (40–55 µm for CoWoS)
  • Independent DVFS — requires per-chiplet VRM or IVR with isolation

Per-Chiplet Voltage Regulation

  • Option A — board VRM per chiplet (separate power domain from PCB)
  • Option B — PIVR per chiplet (Intel Ponte Vecchio approach)
  • Option C — shared VRM + on-die LDO per chiplet (AMD MI300X)
  • Trade-off: board VRM is efficient but slow; on-die LDO is fast but wastes ~5–10%
  • Emerging: GaN-based package-integrated regulators for per-chiplet fast regulation

Power Gating Coordination

  • Inrush current — enabling a power-gated chiplet draws 50–200A in 100 ns
  • Sequencing — stagger chiplet wake-up to limit aggregate di/dt
  • Retention — state retention power gating keeps SRAM live at lower voltage
  • Protocol — chiplet power manager communicates with global power controller via sideband
  • AMD MI300X: 8 XCD + 4 IOD chiplets with coordinated power management
