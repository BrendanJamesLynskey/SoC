The Thermal Challenge

  • Power density — modern AI accelerators exceed 1 W/mm² locally; NVIDIA B200 dissipates 1000 W from ~814 mm² of active die area
  • Junction temperature — Tj,max typically 100–105 °C for datacenter GPUs; every 10 °C rise doubles failure rate (Arrhenius: AF = eΔEa/k(1/T1−1/T2))
  • Thermal runaway — leakage current rises exponentially with temperature → positive feedback loop; at 7 nm, leakage is ~30% of total power at Tj = 100 °C
  • Reliability — electromigration, NBTI, HCI all accelerate with temperature; solder joint fatigue from thermal cycling limits package lifetime to ~60k power cycles
"Thermal management, not transistor density, is the primary limiter of AI accelerator performance scaling."
— TSMC OIP 2024 keynote
Power trend: A100 400 W → H100 700 W → B200 1000 W → Rubin (2026) ~1400 W projected

Thermal Fundamentals

Heat Transfer Mechanisms

Three Modes of Heat Transfer

  • Conduction — Q = kAΔT/L; dominant within die, TIM, heatsink. Silicon k = 130 W/m·K, copper k = 400 W/m·K
  • Convection — Q = hAΔT; forced air h = 25–250 W/m²·K, liquid h = 500–10,000 W/m²·K
  • Radiation — Q = εσA(T4s−T4surr); negligible inside server (<2% of total), relevant only for exposed outdoor enclosures

Thermal RC Model

  • Rth = temperature rise per watt (K/W or °C/W)
  • Cth = thermal mass (J/K); determines transient response
  • Time constant τ = Rth × Cth; silicon die τ ≈ 1–10 ms, heatsink τ ≈ 10–100 s

Thermal Interface Materials (TIM)

TIM fills microscopic air gaps between mating surfaces. Without TIM, contact resistance dominates: bare metal-to-metal leaves ~99% of area as air gaps (kair = 0.025 W/m·K).

Intel uses STIM (indium) on server CPUs; NVIDIA uses high-performance paste or STIM depending on SKU.

TIM2: IHS → Heatsink

  • Thermal paste — most common; 3–12 W/m·K; prone to pump-out (squeeze-out under thermal cycling) and dry-out (volatile loss over months)
  • Phase-change pads — solid at room temp, melt at 45–55 °C; self-healing on each power cycle; 3–6 W/m·K
  • Graphite pads — compressible, reusable; in-plane k > 1000 W/m·K but through-plane only 10–15 W/m·K
  • Liquid metal — gallium-based; 40+ W/m·K; corrosive to aluminium; requires nickel plating on heatsink
Degradation: TIM pump-out increases Rth by 20–50% over 3–5 years in datacenter. STIM eliminates this failure mode entirely.

Air Cooling

Heatsinks · Vapour chambers · Fan design · Server airflow management

05

Heatsink Design

Fin Geometry & Materials

  • Copper baseplate — k = 400 W/m·K; superior spreading but 3.3× heavier and 5× costlier than aluminium
  • Aluminium fins — k = 205 W/m·K; extruded or bonded-fin construction; 60–80% of datacenter heatsinks
  • Fin density — higher FPI (fins per inch) increases surface area but raises pressure drop; optimal: 14–22 FPI for 1U/2U servers
  • Fin height — diminishing returns above ~50 mm due to thermal boundary layer growth; effectiveness η drops below 60%
Rspreading
For 60×60 mm die on 120×120 mm base: ~0.02–0.05 °C/W in copper, ~0.05–0.12 °C/W in aluminium
Rconv
Forced air at 3 m/s: 0.08–0.15 °C/W for a 2U server heatsink

Heat Pipe Integration

  • Sintered heat pipes — Qmax = 30–80 W per 6 mm pipe; effective k > 10,000 W/m·K along length
  • U-shape / L-shape — transport heat from baseplate to remote fin stacks; enables offset designs for RAM clearance
  • Gravity-dependent: performance degrades 10–30% in adverse orientation (evaporator above condenser)
Air cooling limit: practical maximum ~350–400 W for a 2U server heatsink at 35 °C inlet, 80 dBA acoustic budget. Beyond this, liquid cooling becomes necessary.

Fan & Airflow Design

CFD-Optimised Airflow

  • Airflow requirement — ~100–200 CFM per GPU at 700 W; total server (8-GPU): 800–1600 CFM
  • Pressure drop — dense fin arrays create 0.5–2.0 inches H2O static pressure; requires high-static-pressure fans (counter-rotating or multi-stage)
  • Push-pull — dual fan walls increase effective static pressure by ~60%; standard in DGX-style 8-GPU servers
  • CFD validation — ANSYS Icepak / 6SigmaET simulations validated with IR thermography and thermocouple data; typical accuracy ±3 °C
Fan power
200–600 W per server (5–10% of total system power); proportional to RPM3
Acoustic
70–85 dBA at 1 m for datacenter; ≤40 dBA for workstation

Server Rack Airflow Management

  • Front-to-back — standard airflow direction; cold air from cold aisle, exhaust to hot aisle
  • Air recirculation — primary failure mode; hot exhaust re-entering intake raises Tinlet by 5–15 °C; containment curtains or doors eliminate this
  • Blanking panels — seal unused U-spaces; missing panels can raise intake temp by 8 °C
  • Dust filtration — MERV-8 minimum for datacenter; high-altitude sites (Mexico City, Denver) need MERV-11+ due to particulate density
Air cooling economics: fan power at full speed (85 dBA) for an 8×H100 DGX system consumes ~500 W — 7% overhead. Liquid cooling reduces this to ~50 W (pumps), saving 450 W per node × 1000s of nodes.

Cold Plate Liquid Cooling

Direct-to-Chip Cold Plates

  • Copper cold plate — brazed or skived-fin internal channels; turbulent flow at Re > 4000 provides h = 5,000–15,000 W/m²·K
  • Microchannel designs — channel width 50–500 μm; Rth < 0.02 °C/W for 700 W class; limited by pressure drop (0.5–3 bar)
  • Jet impingement — coolant jets directly on cold plate surface; h up to 25,000 W/m²·K; used in custom HPC systems

Direct Liquid Cooling (DLC)

DLC delivers coolant directly to the chip package, bypassing heatsinks and airflow entirely. TSMC's direct-to-silicon approach achieves 0.055 °C/W at 2.6 kW — 3× better than the best air-cooled solutions.

Rear-Door Heat Exchangers (RDHx)

  • Passive RDHx — water coil on rack rear door; cools exhaust air by 15–25 °C; handles 30–50 kW/rack without facility CRAC changes
  • Active RDHx — integrated fans + coil; up to 60 kW/rack; Schneider NetShelter, Vertiv Liebert
  • Neutral air — exhaust exits at room temperature; eliminates hot aisle containment requirement
TSMC breakthrough (2024): direct-to-silicon liquid cooling with microchannels etched into the silicon backside; demonstrated 2.6 kW dissipation with Rth = 0.055 °C/W — enables next-gen 1500 W+ accelerators.

Immersion Cooling

Single-Phase Immersion

  • Principle — servers submerged in dielectric fluid (mineral oil, synthetic ester, or engineered fluids); natural or forced convection removes heat
  • h coefficient — 200–1500 W/m²·K (natural convection in dielectric); modest but uniform
  • Vendors — GRC (ElectroSafe), LiquidCool Solutions, Submer, Asperitas
  • Capacity — 100–200 kW per tank; suitable for racks up to 100 kW

Two-Phase Immersion

  • Boiling heat transfer — fluid boils on chip surface; h = 5,000–20,000 W/m²·K; latent heat absorption is extremely efficient
  • Fluid — 3M Novec 7100/649 (Tboil = 49–61 °C), Engineered Fluids ElectroCool; GWP concerns driving new chemistries
  • Condenser — vapour rises, condenses on water-cooled coils above tank; drips back (passive, no pumps for dielectric)

On-Die Thermal Management

Hotspot Mitigation

Non-Uniform Power Maps

  • Power map variance — tensor cores can hit 2–3 W/mm² locally while SRAM regions run at 0.3 W/mm²; hotspot-to-average ratio of 5–8× is common
  • On-die thermal sensors — 16–64 distributed diode sensors per die; sampled at 1–10 kHz; ring oscillator sensors provide finer spatial granularity
  • Thermal sensor accuracy — ±1–3 °C after one-time calibration at wafer sort; digital readout via ADC with 10–12 bit resolution
Sensor count
H100: 32 thermal diodes; MI300X: 48 per XCD; Gaudi 3: ~24
Response time
Silicon thermal time constant: 1–10 ms; sensor polling must be ≤ 1 ms for effective control

Mitigation Strategies

  • Floorplan optimisation — interleave high-power (compute) and low-power (SRAM, I/O) blocks; avoid clustering tensor cores
  • Dynamic workload migration — OS/firmware redistributes threads away from hot regions; effective for multi-core CPUs, less so for fixed-function accelerators
  • Power gating rotation — temporarily disable hotspot SM/CU clusters and redistribute work to cooler units; 5–10% throughput cost
  • HBM/logic thermal coupling — in 2.5D packages (CoWoS), HBM stacks (Tj,max = 95 °C for HBM3e) are heated by adjacent logic die; thermal guard rings and interposer-level heat spreading required
HBM thermal limit: HBM3e junction limit is 95 °C (JEDEC), only 10 °C above nominal — tighter than the logic die. HBM is often the first component to trigger throttling on 2.5D AI accelerators.
12

Thermal Throttling

Hardware Throttling Mechanisms

  • PROCHOT# — bidirectional signal; asserted when Tj exceeds thermal control threshold (e.g., 97 °C); triggers immediate frequency reduction
  • Thermal trip — hard shutdown at Tj = 105–115 °C; non-maskable; prevents silicon damage; hysteresis of 10–20 °C before restart
  • DVFS throttling — reduce Vcore and frequency together; P ∝ V²f, so 10% frequency reduction yields ~27% power reduction
  • Clock gating — disable clocks to idle functional units; fine-grained (per-SM) in modern GPUs; response time < 1 μs

3D Stacking Thermal Challenges

Inter-Die Thermal Resistance

  • Die-to-die bonding — hybrid bonding (Cu-Cu): Rth = 0.5–2.0 °C·mm²/W; micro-bump (solder): Rth = 5–15 °C·mm²/W; underfill thermal conductivity: 0.3–0.7 W/m·K
  • TSV thermal paths — copper TSVs (k = 400 W/m·K) act as thermal vias; at 10 μm pitch and 30% area coverage, effective through-silicon conductivity doubles
  • Bottom die trapped heat — in face-to-face bonding, bottom die has no direct path to heatsink; heat must flow through top die or through substrate (Rth,sub > 5× Rth,top)
HBM3e stack
12-high die stack; Rth from base to top DRAM ≈ 4–8 °C/W; limits per-stack power to ~20 W
SoIC thermal
TSMC SoIC bond pitch < 10 μm; Rth improved 3–5× over micro-bump

Solutions for 3D Thermal

  • Backside power delivery (BSPDN) — frees frontside for cooling; Intel PowerVia, TSMC N2P backside; reduces Rjc by thinning the thermal path
  • Thermal TSVs — dedicated copper pillars (40–100 μm diameter) placed in non-functional areas; 20–40% Rth reduction
  • Microfluidic inter-die cooling — research: IMEC/Georgia Tech demonstrated microchannels between bonded dies; coolant flows between die layers; Rth < 0.5 °C/W at 250 W/cm²
  • Foveros thermal limit — Intel Foveros currently limited to ~1 W/mm² combined stack power; active cooling between layers needed for >3D stacks
The 3D thermal wall: without inter-die cooling, 3D stacks are limited to ~200 W total. Microfluidic and backside cooling are critical enablers for true 3D compute scaling.

Hot/Cold Aisle Containment

  • Cold aisle containment — enclose cold aisle with doors + roof; prevents mixing; reduces CRAC energy by 20–30%; supply air 18–24 °C
  • Hot aisle containment — enclose hot aisle; exhaust directly to return plenum; allows higher supply temps (27 °C+) for more free cooling hours
  • Chimney cabinets — vertical exhaust ducts on rack top; direct hot air to ceiling plenum; popular in retrofit scenarios

Liquid-to-Liquid vs Liquid-to-Air

  • Liquid-to-air — dry coolers on rooftop; no water consumption; limited by ambient wet-bulb temperature; viable to ~40 kW/rack
  • Liquid-to-liquid — cooling towers or evaporative condensers; higher efficiency; WUE of 0.5–1.5 L/kWh; required for >60 kW/rack in hot climates

AI Supercomputer Cooling

NVIDIA DGX SuperPOD

  • DGX B200 — 8×B200 GPUs × 1000 W = 8 kW GPU + ~4 kW system = ~12 kW per node
  • SuperPOD — 32–64 DGX nodes; 384–768 kW total; requires DLC (CoolIT or Vertiv manifolds)
  • NVLink domain — 72 GPUs connected via NVLink Switch; cable tray thermal management adds 2–5 kW

  • TPU v5p pod — 8,960 chips; estimated 2–3 MW total; liquid-cooled with proprietary cold plates
  • TPU v6 (Trillium) — 4× performance/watt improvement claimed; still liquid-cooled; pod scale increasing

Meta Grand Teton

  • Open Rack v3 — 48V power shelf; designed for 100 kW/rack; OCP-standard liquid cooling manifolds

Case Studies

NVIDIA H100 / B200

  • H100 SXM: 700 W, vapour chamber heatsink, air-cooled baseline; DLC option with CoolIT cold plate (Rth = 0.04 °C/W)
  • B200: 1000 W, DLC mandatory; dual-die (2×Blackwell) on CoWoS-L; custom liquid manifold per baseboard
  • Grace-Blackwell NVL72: 72 GPUs in liquid-cooled rack; ~120 kW/rack

AMD MI300X

  • 750 W TDP; 12 chiplets (8 XCD + 4 IOD) on CoWoS-equivalent; massive 5,332 mm² package
  • Custom vapour chamber spans full OAM module footprint; Rspreading < 0.01 °C/W
  • HBM3 stacks at package edges reduce thermal coupling; THBM managed to ≤89 °C under load

Intel Ponte Vecchio

  • 600 W TDP; 47 tiles on Foveros + EMIB; base tile and compute tiles in 3D stack
  • Liquid cooled from launch (Aurora supercomputer); cold plate with microchannel inserts; Rth,total ≈ 0.055 °C/W
  • Thermal challenge: non-uniform power across 47 tiles; FEM-guided tile placement optimisation

TSMC Direct-to-Silicon

  • Microchannels etched into silicon backside (50 μm wide, 200 μm deep); coolant flows directly over active die
  • Demonstrated 2.6 kW with Rth = 0.055 °C/W; ΔTj = 143 °C → Tj ≤ 180 °C (test) / designed for Tj ≤ 90 °C at 1 kW
  • Roadmap: production-ready by 2026–2027; enables >1500 W accelerators on N2/A16 process
